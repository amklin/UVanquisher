  • My UV sensor isn't working properly. What am I doing wrong?
    The most common source of issue with new users is improper orientation of the wearable sensor. Make sure that the UV sensor can make direct contact with the sunlight for the best and most accurate results.
  • What is UV radiation?
    UV radiation is a form of energy emitted by the sun, coming in three types, UVA, UVB, and UVC, that differ in energy levels. Because the UVC and UVB are mostly absorbed by the ozone layer, UVA is the most prevalent to our daily lives. UV radiation is known to damage DNA in the body's cells, primarily skin cells. For more information, click here.
  • Why is UV radiation dangerous?
    Extended exposure to UV radiation can lead to an abundance of health issues. Obviously, it can cause skin-related issues, including premature aging, wrinkles, sunburn, actinic keratosis, and solar elastosis. Most notoriously, UV radiation is known to cause skin cancer.
  • What do the numbers mean?
    As stated by the EPA, the UV index scale is divided into 5 exposure categories: low, moderate, high, very high, and extreme. Low exposure (<2) means that being outside in the sunlight is very safe. Moderate exposure (3-5) means that being outside in the sunlight is still relatively safe, but you should take precautions like wearing a hat, sunglasses, and sunscreen. High exposure (6-7) means that protection against exposure is needed. Very high and extreme exposure (8-11 & 11+) are very similar in the way that being outside during the day is incredibly dangerous and should be avoided if possible, even with adequate protection.
  • What is skin cancer?
    Skin cancer is characterized by abnormal growth of skin cells. Generally, this will occur in places that are most often exposed to UV radiation, but it can also appear in other places. For more information on skin cancer, click here.
  • When am I at risk for skin cancer?
    Cumulative and extensive sun exposure at high UV levels raise your risk of developing skin cancer drastically. Furthermore, people with fair complexions are often at an even greater risk for skin cancer. When reading your logged data, you should aim to reduce exposure.
